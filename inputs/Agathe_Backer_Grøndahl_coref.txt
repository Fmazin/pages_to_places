[1]Agathe Ursula Backer Grøndahl (1 December 1847 – 4 June 1907) was a Norwegian pianist and composer.
[2]Agathe Backer Grøndahl son Fridtjof Backer-Grøndahl (1885–1959) was also a pianist and composer, who promoted Agathe Backer Grøndahl compositions in his concerts.
[3]


== Biography ==
Agathe Backer Grøndahl was born in Holmestrand in 1847, in a wealthy and art-loving home, as the second youngest of four sisters, all gifted in drawing and music.
[4]In 1857 Agathe Backer Grøndahl moved with Agathe Backer Grøndahl's family to Christiania, where Agathe Backer Grøndahl studied with Otto Winther-Hjelm, Halfdan Kjerulf and Ludvig Mathias Lindeman.
[5]Between 1865 and 1867 Agathe Backer Grøndahl became a pupil of Theodor Kullak and studied composition under Richard Wuerst at the Akademie der Tonkunst in Berlin, where Agathe Backer Grøndahl lived together with Agathe Backer Grøndahl's sister Harriet Backer.
[6]Agathe Backer Grøndahl won fame there with Agathe Backer Grøndahl's interpretation of Beethoven's "Emperor" Concerto.
[7]

[8]After Agathe Backer Grøndahl's arrival to Norway in 1868, Agathe Backer Grøndahl debuted with Edvard Grieg, then 26 years old, as conductor of the Philharmonic Society.
[9]A recommendation from Ole Bull led to further studies with Hans von Bülow in Florence in 1871.
[10]Later the same year Agathe Backer Grøndahl played at the Gewandhaus in Leipzig, becoming a pupil of Franz Liszt in Weimar in 1873.
[11]In 1874 Agathe Backer Grøndahl married the conductor and singing teacher Olaus Andreas Grøndahl, and was generally known thereafter as Agathe Backer Grøndahl.
[12]During the second half of the 1870s Agathe Backer Grøndahl built up an outstanding pianist career with a series of concerts in the Nordic countries, also playing with very great success in London and Paris.
[13]


[14]In 1889 and 1890 Agathe Backer Grøndahl gave concerts in London and Birmingham with a wide-ranging program, including Grieg's piano concerto.
[15]After that Agathe Backer Grøndahl was proclaimed one of the century's greatest piano artists by George Bernard Shaw, who also remarked on the sensitiveness, symmetry and artistic economy of Agathe Backer Grøndahl's compositions.
[16]At the World Exhibition in Paris in 1889, Agathe Backer Grøndahl repeated Agathe Backer Grøndahl's success with Agathe Backer Grøndahl's brilliant interpretation of Grieg's piano concerto.
[17]It was then that Agathe Backer Grøndahl began experiencing nerve problems, although Agathe Backer Grøndahl eventually resumed Agathe Backer Grøndahl's artistic career as a pianist.
[18]Later in the 1890s Agathe Backer Grøndahl became almost completely deaf.
[19]Agathe Backer Grøndahl gave Agathe Backer Grøndahl's last concerts in Sweden and Finland in the autumn of 1901.
[20]Then Agathe Backer Grøndahl retired to teaching.
[21]

[22]As a teacher Agathe Backer Grøndahl was markedly successful and influential.
[23]Agathe Backer Grøndahl students included Erika Stang and Bertha Tapper.
[24]Agathe Backer Grøndahl children studied under Agathe Backer Grøndahl's and are counted with Agathe Backer Grøndahl's gifted pupils.
[25]Agathe Backer Grøndahl was the author of many songs and a great deal of music for the piano, and both as pianist and composer stands at the head of modern music in Norway.
[26]Agathe Backer Grøndahl died at Agathe Backer Grøndahl's home in Ormøya, outside Christiania, at the age of 59.
[27]Agathe Backer Grøndahl is today chiefly remembered for Agathe Backer Grøndahl's piano pieces and songs.
[28]



[29]=== Compositional style ===
Agathe Backer Grøndahl played a major role in the period often called the golden age of Norwegian music history.
[30]Agathe Backer Grøndahl composed in total some 400 pieces spanning seventy opus numbers.
[31]Agathe Backer Grøndahl was a prominent character on the Norwegian musical scene and a close friend of Edvard Grieg.
[32]Agathe Backer Grøndahl earlier compositions synthesized the predominant pianistic and stylistic ideas of 1850s Europe.
[33]In Agathe Backer Grøndahl's later years however, Agathe Backer Grøndahl's style transformed and anticipated some of the early twentieth century impressionistic ideas, which ultimately led the composer Pauline Hall to call Agathe Backer Grøndahl's the first true Norwegian impressionist.
