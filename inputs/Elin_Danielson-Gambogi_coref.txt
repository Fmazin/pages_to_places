[1]Elin Kleopatra Danielson-Gambogi (3 September 1861 – 31 December 1919) was a Finnish painter, best known for Elin Danielson-Gambogi's realist works and portraits.
[2]Elin Danielson-Gambogi was part of the first generation of Finnish women artists who received professional education in art, the so-called "painter sisters' generation".
[3]The group also included Helene Schjerfbeck (1862–1946), Helena Westermarck (1857-1938), and Maria Wiik (1853-1928).
[4]


== Biography ==


=== Early life and studies ===
Elin Danielson-Gambogi was born in the small village of Noormarkku, near the city of Pori in Western Finland.
[5]Elin Danielson-Gambogi was the first-born child of Karl Danielson and Rosa Amalia Danielson, who both came from families of officers and officials, a middle-class background.
[6] 
[7]Elin Danielson-Gambogi early years were spent on a family farm, Ala-Sihtola in Ilmajoki.
[8]Because of the Finnish famine of 1866–68, the farm failed and Karl Danielson went bankrupt.
[9]After being forced to sell the farm, Elin Danielson-Gambogi's father committed suicide.
[10]Elin Danielson-Gambogi mother, Rosa, returned to Noormarkku with her two daughters.
[11]Determined to provide a decent education for her daughters, Rosa worked in a variety of jobs.
[12]Following the tragedy, and surrounded by the strong female figures of Elin Danielson-Gambogi's mother, aunt, and grandmother, Elin Danielson-Gambogi adapted an independent survival strategy.
[13]At the age of 15, Elin Danielson-Gambogi  moved to Helsinki and began studying in the Academy of Fine Arts, where Elin Danielson-Gambogi's teachers included Carl Eneas Sjöstrand and Hjalmar Munsterhjelm.
[14]In 1878, Elin Danielson-Gambogi started courses with Adolf von Becker.
[15]



[16]===
[17]Paris ===
[18]

[19]In 1883 Elin Danielson-Gambogi received a grant and moved to Paris.
[20]While there, Elin Danielson-Gambogi took lessons at the Académie Colarossi under Gustave Courtois and painted in Brittany during the summertime.
[21]A few years later Elin Danielson-Gambogi returned to Finland and lived with Elin Danielson-Gambogi's relatives in Noormarkku and Pori.
[22]In 1888 Elin Danielson-Gambogi opened an atelier in Noormarkku.
[23]During the 1880s and 1890s Elin Danielson-Gambogi worked as a teacher in several art schools around Finland.
[24]Elin Danielson-Gambogi also attended the artists' colony Önningeby in Ålands.
[25]



[26]===
[27]Italy
[28]=
[29]==
In 1895, Elin Danielson-Gambogi received a scholarship and traveled to Florence, Italy.
[30]A year later Elin Danielson-Gambogi moved to the village of Antignano in Livorno where Elin Danielson-Gambogi met an Italian painter 13 years younger than Elin Danielson-Gambogi  Raffaello Gambogi (1874–1943).
[31]They began working together and got married on February 27, 1898.
[32]They held exhibitions in Paris, Florence (where Elin Danielson-Gambogi was awarded an art prize by the city) and Milan, and in many Finnish cities, and their paintings were also included in the 1900 World's Fair in Paris, where Elin Danielson-Gambogi again won bronze medal.
[33]Elin Danielson-Gambogi also got to second place in the 1901 national portrait painting competition organized by the Finnish state.
[34]In 1899, King Umberto purchased a painting from Elin Danielson-Gambogi's 
[35]That same year, Elin Danielson-Gambogi participated in the Venice Biennale.
[36]
Their marriage was strained when Raffaello had an affair with Elin Danielson-Gambogi Finnish friend Dora Wahlroos.
[37]While the affair quickly ended, it had a lasting impact on the Gambogis' marriage.
[38]Elin Danielson-Gambogi moved to Finland for a while, but returned in 1903.
[39]Because of World War I, Elin Danielson-Gambogi's connection to Elin Danielson-Gambogi's homeland was cut, and by the time Elin Danielson-Gambogi died, of pneumonia, at Antignano in 1919, Elin Danielson-Gambogi had been mostly forgotten in Finland.
[40]


== Legacy ==
Because of Elin Danielson-Gambogi's choice of rare subject matters that often even caused some offence, Elin Danielson-Gambogi is now seen as one of the central artists of the Golden Age of Finnish Art.
[41]Danielson-Gambogi was included in the 2018 exhibit Women in Paris 1850-1900.
